The Pacific Rhododendron

Rhododendron Macrophyllum

Common Names

The Pacific Rhododendron is known by many different names, with its scientific name even translating to “rose tree with big leaves.” Below are some of the most commonly used nicknames for this striking and recognizable flower.

  • Coast Rhododendron
  • California Rhododendron
  • California Rosebay
  • Big Leaf Rhododendron
  • Rhodies

Size Ranges

Size can vary widely for the Pacific Rhododendron depending on where it is grown. The typical range can fall anywhere between 5 and 25 feet tall, with the average mature height landing at around 10 to 15 feet. If grown in shaded areas, they can reach their higher potential and become more tree-like in appearance. However, in sunnier conditions, they often remain more compact and short like bushes. In extreme environments, some varieties can be found as small as an inch or even as tall as 100 feet!

Environments

While rhodies are native to the Pacific Northwest, found west of the Cascade Mountains from Northern California to British Columbia, they can also be spotted along coastal areas, including the Coast Mountains and parts of the Cascade Range. They thrive at elevations from sea level all the way up to 6,000 feet, especially with nice damp soil. Surprisingly enough, this hardy flower also seems to enjoy disturbed areas such as roadside shoulders or recently deforested areas. However, they have also been known to tolerate drier conditions as well! Rhododendron have even been found alongside red cedar, hemlocks, Douglas fir, and understory plants like salal and Oregon grape.

If you looking to find native Pacific Rhododendron, the best locations to scout would be the Hood Canal and Olympic Peninsula in Washington state. The beautifully bright blooms are known in the area to create such vibrant landscapes along highways and forest trails.

Washington State Flower

1892 marked the year where Washington chose the Pacific Rhododendron as the state flower. Beating out six other contenders, it was chosen by a group of women to represent the state at the 1893 World Fair hosted in Chicago after the planning for the National Garland of Flowers revealed Washington had not yet designated one for the line-up. Around 15,000 women across the state helped participate in the vote due to the rule of only women being allowed to cast ballots for the decision at the time. The choice was then approved by the state Senate in time to make the exhibition. However, it was only recognized informally as the state flower until it was officially designated by the Washington Legislature in 1949.
